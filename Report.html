IDB open innovation: A guide

Briony Phillips & Benjamin Cave, October 2016

Open innovation has been defined as

’... the use of purposive inflows and outflows of knowledge to accelerate internal innovation, and expand the markets for external use of innovation, respectively’ 

(Chesbrough et al., 2006)

In this paper we consider the merits of 5 different open innovation approaches:

  • Ideathon - online/in person activity to generate innovative ideas in response to an issue/opportunity
  • Hackathon - physical event at which coders build prototype apps/products/services
  • Mapathon - online/in person citizen science style activity at which citizens help create updated mapping resources (particularly to aid humanitarian aid)
  • Datathon - physical event at which data scientists lend their time to help solve specific issues for organisations lacking that expertise (particularly NGOs)
  • Challenge Prize - multi-stage process (online and in person) in which a cash reward is offered to incentivise multidisciplinary teams to meet a defined challenge (can first or most effectively)

The final aspect of this paper proposes a framework to help an individual or organisation quickly select an open innovation technique to fulfil specific objectives and/or opportunity.

Ideathon                                                                                        

Definition:

A virtual or face to face activity designed to generate new ideas in response to a particular user need. Often involves specific idea generation activities and/or group work.

Generalised Objectives:

  • Tap into the intellectual capital (wisdom and imagination) of employees/customers/citizens
  • Engage a community more deeply in your work
  • Create a shared sense of purpose
  • Crowdsource a long list of ideas which can be adopted for development

Audience:

People who are engaged with the subject matter in some way. The more diverse the skills in the audience, the better.

Method:

  • Brief is set by the organising group
  • Activities/prompts/information are provided to encourage generation of ideas
  • Individuals or teams submit their idea
  • Optional: Ideas are refined and best ones are selected/rewarded

Topic: Whole organization or broad area

Participant time commitment: 1 hour – 1 day for participant involvement

Duration of Ideathon: 2 hours - 2 days

Types of Ideathon:

Simple - generation and collection of ideas in response to a specific challenge/user need

Ideathon plus - Simple ideathon combined with challenge prize i.e. incentive to implement idea. This engages a very different audience of entrepreneurs/innovators rather than citizens.

Outputs:

Innovative ideas from a range of perspectives that don’t necessarily conform to organisational norms.

Ideathons traditionally do not generate working prototypes or viable products.

Outcomes:

Community of problem solvers who are engaged with brand/cause

Greater degree of investment (in the problem and solutions) from a wide range of stakeholders

Sample Selection criteria:

Innovation

Sustainability

Impact

Feasibility

Issues to consider:

Ideathons can encounter challenges with intellectual property rights. Whilst participants may be willing to engage with the problem and often enjoy the process of idea generation, they may expect to have a stake in the product or service once it’s developed.

Hackathon

Definition:

A hackathon (also known as a hack day, hackfest or codefest) is an event in which computer programmers and others involved in software development, including graphic designers, interface designers and project managers, collaborate intensively on software projects.[1]

History:

The first recorded hackathon was run in 1999 by OpenBSD in Calgary - it was a cryptographic development event where 10 developers came together to avoid legal problems caused by export regulations of cryptographic software from the United States. At the same time, the term was used independently by Sun Microsystems to refer to an event at the JavaOne conference where John Gage challenged attendees to write a program in Java for the new Palm V using the infrared port to communicate with other Palm users and register it on the Internet.

Since then, hackathons have become increasingly widespread - for examples see ‘case studies’ below.

General Objectives:

  • Quickly develop new software technologies
  • Locate new areas for innovation and funding
  • Test resources such as data integrity/functionality of API
  • Attendees to learn new skills and build professional networks

Audience: 

Those with technical skills including computer programmers and others involved in software development, including graphic designers, interface designers and project managers.

Method:

Usually take place at a given location and are loosely facilitated to encourage idea generation and focus on coding/building specific prototypes. Most hackathons culminate in a presentation or pitch to judges and award of fun/discretionary prizes.

Types of Hackathon:

  • For an application type i.e. focus on a particular platform such as mobile apps, a desktop operating system
  • Using a specific programming language, API, or framework
  • For a cause or purpose e.g. improving government
  • As a tribute or a memorial i.e. in memory of computer programmer
  • For a demographic group e.g. teenagers, women, beginners
  • Internal company hackathons e.g. Netflix, Google
  • Developing a single application e.g. operating system, CMS
  • Innovative economic development i.e. to ignite startup community
  • General i.e. with no restrictions

Topic: Focused problem or technical challenge

Participant time commitment: 24 or 48 hours, often intensive and involves working through the night

Duration of Hackathon: 24 or 48 hours

Outputs:

  • Technically functional prototypes or minimum viable products
  • Participants gain personal and professional development
  • Most hackathon participants attend for the benefit of involvement and personal development and are not motivated to create a financially sustainable product or service.

Outcomes:

  • Refined and tested inputs (data, API etc.)
  • Offers of employment/new jobs
  • Improved awareness of a specific issue

Sample Selection Criteria:

  • Impact - How much impact will this have on the person it's developed for? How much will their daily life improve?
  • Practicality - Does the project fit into people's lives? If not, is it reasonably easy for people to adapt to the product? Think "usability".
  • Feasibility - Can this project be realistically implemented/deployed to the public?
  • Creativity & Technical Innovation - How neat is this thing?!

Mapathon

Definition:

A mapathon is a coordinated mapping event. It is often held inside (armchair mapping) but can also be an outside or combined.[2]

Objectives:

To improve the quality, accuracy and content of open mapping files for the primary benefit of humanitarian causes.

Audience:

Mapping and citizen science communities / all welcome

Method:

Armchair mapping - tracing maps from Bing and other satellite imaging sources. Translate hard copy maps to online.

Outside mapping - working in the field to capture mapping information online

Photography or cartography / citizen science

Topic: 

Geographic area and or specific humanitarian disaster/ geographic change

Mapping integrity

Time:

1 hour – 1 day

Outputs:

  • New maps
  • Community/new relationships

Outcomes:

  • Increased awareness of a specific issue/local area

Datathon[a]

Definition: Datathons are high energy, marathon-style events where mission-driven organizations work alongside teams of volunteer data scientists, developers, and designers to use data to gain insight into their programs, the communities they serve and more. All in 48 hours or less.[3]

General Objectives:

  • To provide NGOs with data science capacity and capability so that they can extract full value from their data and solve social, environmental, and community problems.
  • To derive novel and interesting insight from the data of participating NGOs and non-profit organisations.
  • To improve the quality and consistency of the information with which the NGO works.

Audience: Non Government Organisations (NGOs), data scientists, analysts, researchers, academics.

Method: Exploration with the target organisation to scope and frame question, preparation of data, weekend event, presentation of findings/analysis/outputs, integration of findings into everyday work.

Note: “There are weeks of work ahead (of the Datathon) to scope the projects and prep the data followed by months of impact that ripple out after the weekend is over.“[4]

Types of Datathon:

  • Humanitarian/social cause
  • Dataset accuracy/integrity
  • Organisational investment - community engagement, recruitment, innovation, capability

Topic: Mission or task is set by a single organisation and could be driven by social/humanitarian need or by a specific dataset.

Participant time commitment: 1-2 days (could participate for few hours only)

Duration of Datathon: 1-2 days

Outputs[b]:

  • Answers to pressing questions encountered by the subject organisation
  • Visualisations, analyses, graphs or new datasets

Outcomes:

  • New outlook/perspective on the power of data - Datathons offer a powerful introduction to data science for all organisations involved
  • Clearer, common, understanding of how data science can be used to advance their missions
  • Refreshed perspective on the data and its value/use
  • Identify talented participants to fulfil vacant roles

Challenge Prize

Definitions: “a competition that awards a cash prize for the accomplishment of a feat, usually of engineering. Challenge prizes are typically designed to extend the limits of human ability.”[5]

                                                

“Challenge prizes (also called ‘inducement’ prizes) offer a reward to whoever can first or most effectively meet a defined challenge. They act as an incentive for meeting a specific challenge, rather than being a reward for past achievements.”[6]

History: In 1714 The Longitude Act was established to enable the creation of a reward offered by the British government for a simple and practical method for the precise determination of a ship's longitude - the original Longitude Prize. In 1975 the French Government offered a hefty reward of 12,000 francs during the Napoleonic Wars to invite entrepreneurs of the time to improve upon the food preservation methods. The Orteig Prize ($25,000 reward) was offered on May 19, 1919, by New York hotel owner Raymond Orteig to the first allied aviator(s) to fly non-stop from New York City to Paris or vice versa. It attracted no competitors for several years until it was eventually won in 1927. Challenge Prizes have developed as an increasingly popular technique to incentivise and celebrate innovation over the last 20 years with the advent of the X Prize and the establishment of the Centre for Challenge Prizes at Nesta in 2012.

General Objectives:

  • To support and accelerate change in the world through accomplishment of a specific feat
  • To identify pre-existing products and services that respond to a given challenge, learn about the barriers that exist to solving a given challenge
  • To build capacity and capability of innovators, incentivise them to develop new ideas which respond to a given challenge in collaboration with others
  • To generate sustainable products and services with a viable concept, business model and team

Audience: Entrepreneurs, startups, big business, academic institutions and SMEs

Method: Define challenge, compile resources, support idea generation, manage applications, provide incubator support for participants (e.g. mentoring), judge and reward. There are often multiple rounds of shortlisting/semifinals prior to the selection of an overall winner.

Topic: Specific goal set by organising group - should be a challenging issue (social, economic or environmental). Submissions are often shaped by inclusion of certain criteria e.g. must use open data, team must include consortium of business and academic institutions.

Types of Challenge Prize:

  • Organisational Development e.g. Netflix challenge prize to engage employees and develop their skills
  • Global e.g. Longitude Prize
  • Demonstrator to show power of specific input e.g. open data or a given resource
  • Social issue led - e.g. Nesta ageing well challenge
  • Innovation led - achieve something that has not been achieved before e.g. Suborbital flight, Ansari XPrize

Participant time commitment: 1 hour - full time (depending on level of success through process)

Duration of Challenge: 3 – 8 months or 1 - 5 years depending on gravity of challenge

Outputs:

  • Innovative solutions to specific social, economic or environmental challenge
  • Viable products or services with tested business model
  • Engaged community of innovators who are aware of brand/issue
  • Greater number of innovators working towards a common goal (cash incentive catalyses engagement)

Outcomes:

  • Longer term benefits drawn from a network of knowledgeable people who are engaged with the brand/issue
  • Improved awareness of a specific issue amongst participating
  • Increased profile for the challenge issue
  • Identify talented participants for future employment opportunities

What is involved in Open Innovation Evaluation?

As the ‘DG Enterprise and Industry Study’ says, “there is no “magic bullet” in evaluation: no single method that can answer all of the main questions of an evaluation study, and be applied in all types of study.” Consequently, it is essential that any single program should use a mixture of evaluation methods which should be chosen to fit the needs of the particular project.

Evaluation can be defined as the ‘ability to assess how well a project or activity has achieved a set of objectives that were set on it’s inception’. Some authors, such as Polt (1989) provide a useful differentiation between three aspects of evaluation (efficiency, effectiveness and efficacy):

 

  1. Efficiency - how efficiently the programme operated or how the objectives were met i.e. achieving process. This could include management of the activities, communication of objectives and progress and the ability to detect problems in a timely manner.
  1. Cost
  2. Structure
  3. Resources
  4. Organisational

  1. Effectiveness - how far the objectives were met. This is much easier to manage if the objectives are quantitative and verifiable, or if the project can be benchmarked against other similar projects. Effectiveness is usually measured with the help of performance indicators, or KPIs, which can present issues if poorly selected/written to incentivise the wrong outputs over desired outcomes/impact.

  1. Efficacy - how relevant is the programme to the overarching goals that it was designed to achieve/contribute to.
  1. Scope of the content
  2. Achievements relative to overarching goals
  3. Outcomes and impact
  4. Degree of Effectiveness and Efficiency

Of equal importance when providing a comprehensive evaluation is the explanation or narrative that accompanies the above three factors. The explanation is essential to provide insight into how and why the outcomes and outputs were/were not achieved to the desired level and to capture any unintended consequences of the project.

Evaluation Challenges

Erkens et al. (2013) describe some notable challenges in executing evaluation across open innovation projects. In summary, they highlight:

  • The issue of working across multiple teams, organisations or geographical areas - and the need to apply evaluation techniques consistently and openly.
  • Submissions will be informed by a wide range of experience and technical tools which cannot be easily recorded or quantified.
  • The impact of the products and services generated through open innovation can be very hard to quantify - and can extend across a wide geographical and functional remit.

The open innovation organiser can go some way to limit the breadth of evaluation by stipulating clear judging criteria for each activity, and clearly articulating how marks will be awarded. The broader the instruction (or challenge question) and the wider the invitation/call for responses, the more challenging it will be to conduct a comprehensive evaluation.

Setting a schedule for evaluation

A useful summary regarding timing and execution of evaluation is provided in the ‘DG Enterprise and Industry Study.’ The IDB may wish to consider conducting their evaluation activities at different points in the lifecycle of the project: 

  • Ex-ante evaluation: conducted before the implementation of a programme, and focusing on what its objectives are and how (and how far) they should be achieved.
  • Intermediate evaluation: review the progress of the programme, or its achievements at some point into the programme – usually reserved for long-lasting programmes.
  • Real-time evaluation: follow the programme in detail through its operation.
  • Ex-post evaluation: examine the results of the programme after it has been completed (and possibly more than once).

Approaches to Event/Project Evaluation

There are a wide range of evaluation approaches available which are relevant to varying degrees depending on the open innovation technique employed.

As previously discussed, the timing of evaluation activities is crucial to the impact they deliver, and reception they receive. Whilst a post event participant experience survey should be sent out during the event or immediately after, an impact assessment conducted both 12 months and 36 months after the Challenge has been completed will deliver far more insightful data than one conducted immediately after the event.

Furthermore, the purveyor of the evaluation activity can have a significant impact on the uptake, the nature of the response and the impact of the completed report. An independently conducted survey is likely to generate a more honest response than one which is collected by the facilitator at the event.

Kirkpatrick is one of the most widely used and longest-standing evaluation systems. It is structured as follows:

Level 1: Reaction, satisfaction and planned action

Measures participants’ reaction to, and satisfaction with, the program and captures planned actions.

Level 2: Learning

Measures changes in knowledge, skills and attitudes.

Level 3: Application and implementation

Measures changes in on-the-job behavior and progress with planned actions.

Level 4: Business impact

Measures changes in business impact variables.

Level 5: Return on investment

Compares a program’s monetary benefits with the costs of the program.

Business Models for Open Innovation

The title ‘Open Innovation’ infers much, not only about the outcomes which these activities are designed to achieve, but also about the manner in which they are intended to be executed. Open Innovation can only be truly open if the barriers to participation are as low as possible - whether those barriers are cost of travel/entry, running time of the event or complex subject matter.

 

It is for the IDB to determine to what degree they wish to make their events truly open - and which principles they should embed across their work. They may wish to consider the following core principles for example:

-       Our events will be open to all: we will clearly signpost the target audience;

-       Materials/content will be provided to ensure all participants understand the subject matter and the intended broader impact of our activities; and

-       The environment we create will be supportive: catering and materials will always be provided gratis at our events.

Each Open Innovation technique lends itself to specific incentive/sponsorship approaches as we have seen from the case studies provided. The summary below highlights those considerations that should influence the IDB approach.

 

Ideathon

 

Delivery model: Delivered most commonly online using a tech platform such as YouNoodle or bespoke in-house tech. Can also be delivered in person but this limits the pool of ideas and expertise to those who are represented in the room.

Participant profile: Ideathons are the most accessible form of open innovation - a diverse community of participants can be expected. The participants could including those who are domain experts and work in the relevant sector to those who have personal experience of the problem that is posed.

(Innovators, Citizen-innovators, Subject matter experts)

Participant motivation: Primarily cash incentive, can also be driven to solve a particular problem.

 

It is vital to provide a cash incentive - the ideathon relies on participants applying their experience, knowledge and interest to create, develop and submit innovative ideas. Participants usually forgo their intellectual property and any future profit derived from their idea so should be rewarded in some way.

 

Some participants will find brand association/sponsorship to be off-putting to participants - they may not agree with the values/practices of those organisations and/or may feel that their ideas are simply going to contribute to the profits of large corporate organisations. This issue can be overcome if necessary checks and measures are put in place.

 

Hackathon

 

Delivery model: Hackathon events benefit from in person delivery as this provides the opportunity for teams combine according to their skills, interests and experience in a competition environment.

Participant profile: Hackathons attract coders, developers, entrepreneurs and data scientists who are often ambitious and driven.

(Computer programmers, graphic designers, interface designers and project managers)

Participant motivation: Hackathons were originally designed as professional development opportunities to enable community building and development of skills. Increasingly participants are also motivated by the opportunity to secure prizes and develop working prototypes. It is unusual for products of hackathons to be sustainable products and services as the teams are largely not invested in them.

 

Business Model:

As a result, hackathons are widely sponsored by technology companies looking for potential employees and brand exposure. Sponsorship can take any one of a number of guises, including:

●          Logo and brand associated with the hackathon

●          Sponsoring food/beverages

●          Physical contribution to ‘swag bag’ for participants

●          Flyers on the tables

●          Offer prizes (technology devices)/ cash/ money-can’t-buy experiences

 

Mapathon

 

Delivery model: Mapathons are conducted both in person and online (virtual).

Participant profile: Whilst the technical skills required to participate in a mapathon are not complex, these events are associated with more technically minded participants or those who want to contribute to a significant social goal (e.g. mapping an earthquake area).

(Citizens, humanitarians, data scientists)

Participant motivation: Attendees are frequently motivated by a social or environmental cause and participate in order to feel a sense of participation and altruism. They expect hygiene factors to be covered including wifi/refreshments if event is in person.

 

Business Model:

IDB may wish to secure sponsorship in the form of catering or refreshments for participants as these are an incentive for participants.

 

Datathon

 

Delivery model: Datathons are conducted in person. Attendees are frequently motivated by the opportunity to test and develop new skills, by a sense of competition of for altruistic reasons.

Participant profile: Mission-Driven organisations who require a specific data related outcome. Volunteer data scientists, developers, and designers who are often socially motivated and/or wish to build experience or develop specific skills.

(Data scientists, analysts, consultants.)

Participant motivation: Participants are usually socially motivated and attracted to the opportunity to develop experience and skills for their portfolio. Participants value the opportunity to build their personal/professional network.

                                                             

Business Model: A Datathon could be sponsored in entirety by a specific brand (e.g. Kraft Datadive) or should be philanthropically funded in order to support a specific non-profit cause by providing additional capacity and capability where it is not traditionally available. In the latter case, sponsorship may be possible through brand association.          

 

Challenge Prize

 

Delivery model: Challenges are largely delivered through a mix of online and offline activity. The challenge process is deliberately extended over a number of months in order to identify and support the best in class to reach prototype or first customer stage.  

Participant profile: Entrepreneurs, academics/researchers, domain experts – depending on the nature of the challenge.

(Businesses (R&D), SMEs working in the sector, entrepreneurs, start-ups and academic institutions)

Participant motivation: Motivated by the cash incentive and the cerebral challenge of solving an unsolved problem. The size of the cash prize will directly influence the type of participant – smaller prizes will attract students, and the larger the cash prize, the larger the organization likely to participate. Some challenges have a specific social problem at their centre and this acts as an additional incentive.

 

Business Model: Gain contributions to the cash prize from large tech companies. Request sponsorship for specific elements of the process. Opportunity for large companies to recruit/increase brand awareness, coveted sponsorship opportunity. Sponsorship packages including brand association, contribution to cash prize, invitation to judge

IDB Open Innovation Strategy

In order to deliver against the following objectives (derived from the IDB/ODI Workshop, July 2016), the IDB may wish to prioritise the following open innovation activities in coordination with one another.

 

Proposed Objectives:

  • Engage and build relationships with government stakeholders (network)
  • Develop open innovation capacity and capability amongst IDB employees (skills)
  • Catalyse outputs and outcomes of open innovation
  • Build a community of civil actors, innovators, coders and entrepreneurs willing to support IDB

  

Priority Deliverables:

 

  1. IDEATHON - Develop an annual schedule of ideathons (2-3 per year) to Invite IDB employees and general public to respond to a specific call for ideas online. Consider connecting to the ‘human cloud’ in a way which is:
  1. “Participative or Invitational: Should the sponsors invite specific people to submit ideas or should the innovation effort be open to all interested partners?
  2. Suggestive or Directed: Should the ideas be influenced or directed by topics or needs specified by the sponsor, or should the participants be allowed to submit ideas with no asserted boundaries or conditions?” [7]
  1. CHALLENGE - Run one annual challenge prize across all geographic regions in partnership with local accelerators/ incubators or technology companies.
  2. HACKATHON – Build a reputation as conveners of a diverse community of hackers/technology specialists to test datasets and demonstrate their potential. Secure sponsorship from large tech. companies. Develop a recruitment pipeline for IDB.
  3. MAPATHON and Datathon - Work with local causes to convene socially motivated participants and develop useful resources (e.g. maps, data insights/analysis and infographics) to contribute to specific local areas of need.
