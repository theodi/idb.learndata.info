Challenge Prize

Definitions: “a competition that awards a cash prize for the accomplishment of a feat, usually of engineering. Challenge prizes are typically designed to extend the limits of human ability.”[1]

                                                

“Challenge prizes (also called ‘inducement’ prizes) offer a reward to whoever can first or most effectively meet a defined challenge. They act as an incentive for meeting a specific challenge, rather than being a reward for past achievements.”[2]

History: In 1714 The Longitude Act was established to enable the creation of a reward offered by the British government for a simple and practical method for the precise determination of a ship's longitude - the original Longitude Prize. In 1975 the French Government offered a hefty reward of 12,000 francs during the Napoleonic Wars to invite entrepreneurs of the time to improve upon the food preservation methods. The Orteig Prize ($25,000 reward) was offered on May 19, 1919, by New York hotel owner Raymond Orteig to the first allied aviator(s) to fly non-stop from New York City to Paris or vice versa. It attracted no competitors for several years until it was eventually won in 1927. Challenge Prizes have developed as an increasingly popular technique to incentivise and celebrate innovation over the last 20 years with the advent of the X Prize and the establishment of the Centre for Challenge Prizes at Nesta in 2012.

General Objectives:

  • To support and accelerate change in the world through accomplishment of a specific feat
  • To identify pre-existing products and services that respond to a given challenge, learn about the barriers that exist to solving a given challenge
  • To build capacity and capability of innovators, incentivise them to develop new ideas which respond to a given challenge in collaboration with others
  • To generate sustainable products and services with a viable concept, business model and team

Audience: Entrepreneurs, startups, big business, academic institutions and SMEs

Method: Define challenge, compile resources, support idea generation, manage applications, provide incubator support for participants (e.g. mentoring), judge and reward. There are often multiple rounds of shortlisting/semifinals prior to the selection of an overall winner.

Topic: Specific goal set by organising group - should be a challenging issue (social, economic or environmental). Submissions are often shaped by inclusion of certain criteria e.g. must use open data, team must include consortium of business and academic institutions.

Types of Challenge Prize:

  • Organisational Development e.g. Netflix challenge prize to engage employees and develop their skills
  • Global e.g. Longitude Prize
  • Demonstrator to show power of specific input e.g. open data or a given resource
  • Social issue led - e.g. Nesta ageing well challenge
  • Innovation led - achieve something that has not been achieved before e.g. Suborbital flight, Ansari XPrize

Participant time commitment: 1 hour - full time (depending on level of success through process)

Duration of Challenge: 3 – 8 months or 1 - 5 years depending on gravity of challenge

Outputs:

  • Innovative solutions to specific social, economic or environmental challenge
  • Viable products or services with tested business model
  • Engaged community of innovators who are aware of brand/issue
  • Greater number of innovators working towards a common goal (cash incentive catalyses engagement)

Outcomes:

  • Longer term benefits drawn from a network of knowledgeable people who are engaged with the brand/issue
  • Improved awareness of a specific issue amongst participating
  • Increased profile for the challenge issue
  • Identify talented participants for future employment opportunities

Case Studies

1. Open Data Challenge Prizes, Nesta and ODI, UK 2013 - 2015 (Funded by Department for Business, Innovation and Skills)

Format: 2 phase open data challenge. Challenge definition, data sharing, submission, selection of semi finalists, creation weekend, selection of finalists, incubation, selection of winner

Topic: Range of 7 social issues ranging from education to heritage and culture.

Incentive: £5,000 for semi finalists + mentoring, £40 - 50,000 for final winner

Goal: To develop sustainable, innovative products and services using open data for social good. To engage entrepreneurs and innovators in open data.

Judging Criteria: Sustainable, use of open data, innovative, social impact.

2. Longitude Prize, Nesta, 2014 - 2019 (Funding partner: InnovateUK)

Format: 3 phase challenge. Challenge question researched and selected from 6 options by public vote. Registration, application, judging (Prize Advisory Committee), testing, judging (Longitude Committee), winner declared. If necessary, this cycle will be completed multiple times until a winner is identified and verified.

Discovery awards available to provide seed funding to develop an idea.

Topic: How can we prevent the rise of resistance to antibiotics? (On average antibiotics add 20 years to each person’s life. The development of antibiotics has been vital to our survival, yet the rise of antimicrobial resistance is threatening to make them ineffective in the future.)

Incentive: £10 Million

Goal: To engage a diverse community and solve one of the greatest challenges of our time

Judging criteria: Needed, accurate, affordable, rapid, easy-to-use, scalable, safe, connected, prototype.

Nesta is home of the Centre for Challenge Prizes and runs 2-3 challenges annually with smaller cash prizes.

3. Ansari XPRIZE, funded by the Ansari family,

Format: Began with $2.5 million in seed money, which led to a $10 million prize purse. Over the course of the competition, 26 teams from 7 nations invested over $100 million in aggregate for research and development in suborbital space flight.

Topic: Suborbital spaceflight. Teams from around the world were challenged to build a reliable, reusable, privately financed, manned spaceship capable of carrying three people to 100 kilometers above the Earth's surface twice within two weeks.

Incentive: $10 million (largest prize in history) won by Richard Branson for Virgin Galactic

Goal: Incentivise the creation of a safe, reliable, reusable, privately-financed manned spaceship to demonstrate that private space travel is commercially viable.

“XPRIZE is an innovation engine. A facilitator of exponential change. A catalyst for the benefit of humanity.” They identify the problem and incentivise the world to solve it. Their challenges are audacious, but achievable, clearly explained so everyone can participate and tied to objective, measurable goals. Breakthroughs made as a result of this successful Ansari XPRIZE competition led to a private space industry worth over $2 billion today.

4. NASA’s Centennial Challenge, NASA, Space Technology Mission Directorate (STMD)

Format: Prizes involves multiple rounds and are initially advertised online. Complex schedule which includes physical events to bring teams together and select finalists/winners, often multiple opportunities to qualify for seed funding and involvement in further rounds.

Topic: Advanced technology development

Incentive: Ranges from US$25,000 - US$5 Million prize purse

Goal: To engage the public and particularly citizen-inventors in NASA’s research and development. Innovation (find innovative solutions through competition and cooperation), opportunity (enable Challenge competitors to develop and/or expand business models and business base) and communication (provide a forum for public outreach).

Initiated in 2005 to directly engage the public in the process of advanced technology development. The programme offers incentive prizes to generate revolutionary solutions to problems of interest to NASA and the nation. The programme seeks innovations from diverse and non-traditional sources. Competitors are not supported by government funding and awards are only made to successful teams when the challenges are met. The prize contests are named "Centennial" in honor of the 100 years since the Wright brothers' first flight in 1903. The Wright Brothers' pioneering inventions embody the spirit of the challenges.

The President’s budget request includes $4 million per year for Centennial Challenges prizes to allow further growth in the scope and range of prize competitions and even greater opportunities for the citizen-inventor to participate in NASA’s research and development. There are currently 3 live challenges including: Mars Ascent Vehicle Prize and Cube Quest Challenge. Competitions are managed by independent, non-profit organisations. NASA provides the Centennial Challenge prize money.
