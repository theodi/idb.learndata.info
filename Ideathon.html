Ideathon                                                                                        

Definition:

A virtual or face to face activity designed to generate new ideas in response to a particular user need. Often involves specific idea generation activities and/or group work.

Generalised Objectives:

  • Tap into the intellectual capital (wisdom and imagination) of employees/customers/citizens
  • Engage a community more deeply in your work
  • Create a shared sense of purpose
  • Crowdsource a long list of ideas which can be adopted for development

Audience:

People who are engaged with the subject matter in some way. The more diverse the skills in the audience, the better.

Method:

  • Brief is set by the organising group
  • Activities/prompts/information are provided to encourage generation of ideas
  • Individuals or teams submit their idea
  • Optional: Ideas are refined and best ones are selected/rewarded

Topic: Whole organization or broad area

Participant time commitment: 1 hour – 1 day for participant involvement

Duration of Ideathon: 2 hours - 2 days

Types of Ideathon:

Simple - generation and collection of ideas in response to a specific challenge/user need

Ideathon plus - Simple ideathon combined with challenge prize i.e. incentive to implement idea. This engages a very different audience of entrepreneurs/innovators rather than citizens.

Outputs:

Innovative ideas from a range of perspectives that don’t necessarily conform to organisational norms.

Ideathons traditionally do not generate working prototypes or viable products.

Outcomes:

Community of problem solvers who are engaged with brand/cause

Greater degree of investment (in the problem and solutions) from a wide range of stakeholders

Sample Selection criteria:

Innovation

Sustainability

Impact

Feasibility

Issues to consider:

Ideathons can encounter challenges with intellectual property rights. Whilst participants may be willing to engage with the problem and often enjoy the process of idea generation, they may expect to have a stake in the product or service once it’s developed.

Case Studies

1. Knight Foundation News Challenges, USA, Bi-annual deadlines, Knight Foundation

Format: Online call for submissions, initial judging provided openly online,

Topic: How might libraries serve 21st century information needs?

Incentive: Share of $3 million funding and support from Knight’s network of influential peers and advisors to help advance their ideas.

Goals: To incentivise innovative ideas from citizens and specialists that will be implemented

The Knight Foundation regularly announces a specific theme for a specific challenge (most recently related to libraries). Everyone is welcome to submit a response by a given deadline (“Innovators from all industries and countries are invited to participate”) and encouraged to engage others to demonstrate support for their idea through the online platform. Previous winners include leading internet entrepreneurs, emerging media innovators and legacy newsrooms.

The ideathon is conducted in the open with all stages recorded for posterity in their highly informative website: https://www.newschallenge.org/challenge/how-might-libraries-serve-21st-century-information-needs/evaluation. In the case of the most recent challenge, over 600 entries were made, with 51 semifinalists and 14 overall winners.

Participants are encouraged to:

  1. Be visual. You can add images and videos to your posts. This helps everyone understand your submission.
  2. Comment on and applaud other people's entries. Our goal is an ongoing, open, positive conversation. Your feedback and ideas help others improve their work.
  3. Iterate! You can come back as many times as you like to update your entry, building on the comments of other members of the community.

2. "Inspiring Hope" Ideathon, Boston MA, September 2016, INC Research and CISCRP

Format: Participants respond to one of four problem statements by submitting their idea through an online submission

Topic: Increasing awareness of clinical trials amongst patients, healthcare professionals and the general public.

Incentive: Shortlisted teams invited to 2 day event to receive mentoring support, $10,000 prize

Goals: To identify unique, innovative and effective new ideas that can make a difference to the broader community. To build a community of problem solvers. To bring hope to millions of people awaiting new treatment options.

Data suggests less than 5% of people take part in clinical studies. This ideathon is designed to provide a platform to develop an active community that will learn from each other and work toward a common goal to raise public awareness about clinical research and reasons to participate in clinical trials. The ideathon invites biopharmaceutical companies, advocacy and non-profit organizations, physicians and healthcare professionals, marketing organizations, technology providers, and patients themselves to contribute innovative ideas and creativity to generate new and unique approaches.

Ideas can include the use of data and new technology, re-purposing of current technology and crowd solving solutions.

Ideathon is being managed by HeroX: https://herox.com/inspiring-hope-ideathon

3. India Ideathon v3.0, October 2016, CapitalVia

Format: Online call for ideas to be submitted virtually in 500 word description

Topic: Open call for innovative ideas

Incentive: Three cash prizes available ranging from GBP1,200 - GBP280

Goal: To identify and support 3 teams of entrepreneurs to develop their idea to fruition

Sponsored by a variety of big brands and led by CapitalVia.

Designed as a mechanism to identify entrepreneurs and innovations of the future this online ideathon invites participants aged 19 - 55 to submit their ideas openly online. Although advertised as an ideathon, the three phase process culminates in the award of three cash prizes to teams in order to support the implementation of their idea which suggests this is an ideathon/challenge prize hybrid.

This is the third iteration of the India Ideathon and the submission deadline has been postponed to Oct 2016 which suggests a lack of participants. Previous years saw c. 350 submissions in the first stage and c. 130 through to the second phase from which 3 winners were selected after judging scrutiny to identify those ideas most likely to succeed.

I would hypothesise (from the messaging on their website and from my personal experience) that the limited number of participants may be explained by participants concern regarding intellectual property particularly with such a range of corporate sponsors involved.

4. HCI (Human Computer Interaction) for Peace Ideathon, August 2013, CHI 2013

Format: 20 attendees, split into 4 groups for discussions on separate topics

Topic: the role HCI researchers can play in preventing, de-escalating, and recovering from armed conflict

Incentive: None, social cause

Goal: to crowd source information on existing good practice and to generate new ideas

Members of the human-computer interaction (HCI) community interested in using computing technologies to promote peace and prevent conflict met as part of HCI for Peace, a grassroots initiative. HCI for Peace hosted a meeting at CHI (Computer Human Interaction) 2013 in Paris. This special interest group (SIG) was the first opportunity for CHI attendees to meet during the main part of the conference, share their ideas, and provide concrete ways to move forward with this line of research. It was advertised as an "Ideathon" in order to focus attendees on the creation of lots of ideas specifically regarding the role HCI researchers can play in preventing, de-escalating, and recovering from armed conflict.

Academic paper available: http://dl.acm.org/citation.cfm?id=2468819

5. BBC Pop Up Crowdsourcing unit, @BBCPopUp, US, Canada, Kenya, India

Format: Viewers suggest local stories via tweet or conversation that should be filmed

Topic: Geographically determined - the team spends a month in a different geographic location filming stories

Incentive: See your story filmed and available online/used on the BBC

Goal: To engage the local community in storytelling and identify untold stories

BBC Pop Up is the company's crowd-sourcing unit and is filming story ideas sent in by viewers. The team relocates to a new city for a month at a time and attempts to become part of each community. They film story ideas suggested to us by locals or by those on social media.
