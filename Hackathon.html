Hackathon

Definition:

A hackathon (also known as a hack day, hackfest or codefest) is an event in which computer programmers and others involved in software development, including graphic designers, interface designers and project managers, collaborate intensively on software projects.[1]

History:

The first recorded hackathon was run in 1999 by OpenBSD in Calgary - it was a cryptographic development event where 10 developers came together to avoid legal problems caused by export regulations of cryptographic software from the United States. At the same time, the term was used independently by Sun Microsystems to refer to an event at the JavaOne conference where John Gage challenged attendees to write a program in Java for the new Palm V using the infrared port to communicate with other Palm users and register it on the Internet.

Since then, hackathons have become increasingly widespread - for examples see ‘case studies’ below.

General Objectives:

  • Quickly develop new software technologies
  • Locate new areas for innovation and funding
  • Test resources such as data integrity/functionality of API
  • Attendees to learn new skills and build professional networks

Audience: 

Those with technical skills including computer programmers and others involved in software development, including graphic designers, interface designers and project managers.

Method:

Usually take place at a given location and are loosely facilitated to encourage idea generation and focus on coding/building specific prototypes. Most hackathons culminate in a presentation or pitch to judges and award of fun/discretionary prizes.

Types of Hackathon:

  • For an application type i.e. focus on a particular platform such as mobile apps, a desktop operating system
  • Using a specific programming language, API, or framework
  • For a cause or purpose e.g. improving government
  • As a tribute or a memorial i.e. in memory of computer programmer
  • For a demographic group e.g. teenagers, women, beginners
  • Internal company hackathons e.g. Netflix, Google
  • Developing a single application e.g. operating system, CMS
  • Innovative economic development i.e. to ignite startup community
  • General i.e. with no restrictions

Topic: Focused problem or technical challenge

Participant time commitment: 24 or 48 hours, often intensive and involves working through the night

Duration of Hackathon: 24 or 48 hours

Outputs:

  • Technically functional prototypes or minimum viable products
  • Participants gain personal and professional development
  • Most hackathon participants attend for the benefit of involvement and personal development and are not motivated to create a financially sustainable product or service.

Outcomes:

  • Refined and tested inputs (data, API etc.)
  • Offers of employment/new jobs
  • Improved awareness of a specific issue

Sample Selection Criteria:

  • Impact - How much impact will this have on the person it's developed for? How much will their daily life improve?
  • Practicality - Does the project fit into people's lives? If not, is it reasonably easy for people to adapt to the product? Think "usability".
  • Feasibility - Can this project be realistically implemented/deployed to the public?
  • Creativity & Technical Innovation - How neat is this thing?!

Case Studies

1. Over the Air Hackathon, UK, Annual

Format: From Friday AM - Saturday PM, Plenary/keynote to kick off the event.

Topic: Specific challenges are sponsored by specific organisations for the Hackday e.g.use the JustGiving API in a creative way to the benefit of a named charity.

Incentive: Specific prize for each challenge e.g. Arduino Starter Kits with UNO Board

Goal: Professional development for attendees, community building, networking and creativity.

Over the Air is an annual 2-day mobile developer Hack Day, with a mix of workshops to share new technologies, and a hacking competition to celebrate programming & making as a creative discipline. Mobile apps, mobile web, mobile devices, prototyping platforms such as Arduino, mBed & Raspberry Pi, wearable technology, robotics, LEGO Mindstorms …everything and anything, with a focus on connected devices and “things.”

Over the Air is a unique tech-agnostic event for and by the developer community, featuring technical workshops where attendees can roll up their sleeves and tinker with new development platforms, operating systems, APIs, SDKs, hardware and devices.

Although it’s mostly about software development and hardware hacking, you don’t need to be a programmer to attend. Attendees include project managers, product managers, designers, entrepreneurs, students and many more. The common thread is that you are interested in building cool stuff!

“It’s a celebration of creativity. It’s a festival of all things hackable. It’s a place where attendees can learn about technologies, trends and techniques. It’s a sleepover. It’s a two day party. It’s an event where practitioners of creative and technical disciplines can come together to learn from each other.”

2. DementiaHack, UK Government and HackerNest

Format: Friday - Sunday. Panel discussions to explore user need followed by consensus based workshop to synthesise content. 24 hour coding activity to develop prototypes. Judges assessed and selected winner.

Topic: Dementia

Incentive: Demonstration space for DementiaHack winners at the UKHealthTech in addition to facilitated meetings with potential clients.

Goal: Not content to just "raise awareness", the British government is committed to developing the hardware and software prototypes that emerge into tangible, lasting solutions that dementia patients/caregivers will actually benefit from.

Second event being organised in Toronto 2016.

3. Netflix Hack Day, USA, Annual - organisation hack day

Format: 24 hour traditional hack attended by all staff voluntarily (c. 200 people). Staff are given free reign to develop anything they want to.

Topic: Netflix/company related creative ideas

Incentive: Recognition

Goal: Professional development, cross team working, creativity - not necessarily for implementation into the Netflix product offering

4. PennApps, Annual, Pennsylvania

Format: Online application to attend. Friday PM - Sunday PM. Routes, mentoring and workshops throughout.

Topic: Real world problems

Incentive: $60k available in prizes, sponsored by multiple big tech. businesses

Goal: Community building, skills development for attendees, recruitment opportunity for sponsoring companies

PennApps at the University of Pennsylvania was the first student-run college hackathon; in 2015 it became the largest college hackathon with its 12th iteration hosting over 2000 people and offering over $60k in prizes. Teams of up to four people spend the weekend working on innovative software and hardware solutions to real-world problems. These projects range in platform and application, including elements of web development, mobile applications, drones, and more.

PennApps philosophy is that everyone should be able to participate, from individuals who have never written a line of code to experts who have been developing for years. To help facilitate the learning experience, they offer a number of resources to help during the weekend.
